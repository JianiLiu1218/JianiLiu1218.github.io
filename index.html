Research

Work in Progress

  • Entrepreneurial Dynamics and the Great Recession

    • There is significant inequality among self-employed, but why is there a lack of switching? Self-employed income is more sensitive to shocks, so why are entry and exit rates so smooth over time? How, and in what ways, do unemployment insurance (UI) policies affect entrepreneurship? I study occupational choices over time and the economic effects of redistributive policies, focusing on smooth entry rates.

  • Maturity and Consumer Default Risks

    • I study the implications of unsecured debt maturity in a general equilibrium model wherein households face idiosyncratic endowment shocks. In the model economy, households can default on their debts. I find that as maturities lengthen, the number of borrowers grows. The increase in borrowers coincides with an amplified overall default rate and indicates a decrease in savers, culminating in a decline in total savings. While people increasingly engage in borrowing activities, individual households are borrowing less. The comovements of borrowings and savings manifest in equilibrium, where the risk-free rate experiences a slight decrement as maturity extends. I also analyze welfare effects and observe that households prefer long-term debt over short-term debt, contrasting with the findings of partial equilibrium models. The small risk-free rate movements can account for this difference.
